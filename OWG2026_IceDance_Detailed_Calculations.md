## Part 2: The Scoring System — How It Works

### 2.1 ISU International Judging System (IJS)

Under the IJS, nine judges score every technical element. For each element, the scoring process is:

1. **Each judge awards a Grade of Execution (GOE)** — an integer from −5 to +5
2. **The GOE integers are converted to points** by multiplying by a scale factor tied to the element's base value
3. **The highest and lowest GOE scores are dropped** (the "trimmed mean") — this is the structural safeguard built into the system
4. **The remaining 7 scores are averaged** to produce the Panel GOE
5. **Element Score = Base Value + Panel GOE**

### 2.2 Total Segment Score Formula

> **TSS = TES + PCS − Deductions**

Where:
- **TES** (Total Element Score) = Sum of all individual element scores
- **PCS** (Program Component Score) = Panel-scored artistic/skating quality marks
- **Deductions** = Penalties for falls, illegal elements, time violations, etc.

### 2.3 Why the Trimmed Mean Doesn't Catch Everything

Dropping the high and low on each element individually prevents one judge from dominating a single element. However, a judge who is *consistently* the highest (or lowest) scorer for one team and consistently the lowest (or highest) for a rival team will have their scores counted on most elements — they simply won't be the single extreme on every element. This is the governance gap that OSNR is designed to detect.

## Part 5: Detailed Calculations — Rhythm Dance

### How to Read the Element Tables

Each row shows:
- **BV** = Base Value (set by the Technical Panel based on difficulty)
- **J1–J9** = Each judge's integer GOE grade (−5 to +5)
- **Trim GOE** = Trimmed mean of GOE points (high and low dropped; 7 scores averaged)
- **Element Score** = BV + Trim GOE

The GOE scale converts integers to points using a factor proportional to the element's base value. The trimmed mean is computed on the *converted points*, not the raw integers. However, because the conversion factor is constant within an element, dropping the extreme integers and averaging is equivalent to dropping the extreme converted values — the calculation shown is exact.

## Part 7: The OSNR Model — Statistical Analysis

### 7.1 What the Model Measures

The OSNR (Outlier Score Nullification Rule) framework tests whether any judge's scores are *systematically* biased when comparing two competitors across all elements of their programs. The key statistic is:

> **B(j) = Σ[d(u,j) for u ∈ FRA elements] − Σ[d(u,j) for u ∈ USA elements]**

Where **d(u,j)** = judge j's GOE score on element u, minus the panel mean (trimmed mean) for that element. This measures how much judge j is *above or below the consensus* for each team, summed across all elements.

A positive B(j) means judge j is systematically *above* the consensus for FRA and/or *below* the consensus for USA — favorable to FRA relative to the rest of the panel.

The significance of BI(j) is assessed using an **exact combinatorial permutation test**: all of judge j’s peer-median deviations for both competitors are combined into one pool, then every possible way to split that pool into two equal groups is enumerated. For a 9-element program, there are C(18, 9) = 48,620 such splits. The exact p-value is the fraction of those splits that produce a simulated BI(j) as extreme or more extreme than the one observed. The result is fully deterministic — no random shuffling, no seed.

## Part 8: Leave-One-Judge-Out (LOJO) Counterfactual Analysis

### 8.1 What the LOJO Calculates

The LOJO asks: *if this judge had not been on the panel, would the result have been different?*

For each judge removal, the trimmed mean is recomputed using the remaining 8 judges (dropping the 1 highest and 1 lowest of the 8), and all 20 teams are re-ranked. The winning team under the reduced panel is the counterfactual (CF) winner.

### 8.3 Interpreting the LOJO Results

**The margin is thin:** With a 0.97-point official margin across a ~135-point score, the gold is decided by approximately 0.72% of the total score — extremely close by historical standards.

**Convergence without J1 and J3:** Several judges, when removed, compress the margin toward zero (J4: +0.16, J3: −0.17), suggesting the panel as a whole was nearly split. The full nine-judge panel produced an outcome that reversed when any one of three judges was removed.

**The OSNR trigger:** Under the two-tier OSNR rule:
- **Tier 1 (flag at p ≤ 0.01):** J1 DABOUIS triggers a Tier 1 flag (p = 0.00127)
- **Tier 2 (automatic adjustment at p ≤ 0.001 AND outcome-determinative):** J1's p-value of 0.00127 falls just *above* the Tier 2 threshold of p ≤ 0.001. Under current OSNR parameters, this would generate a flag for review but not an automatic score adjustment.
