## 1. Expert Witness and Legal Consulting

### What Types of Cases Need This Expertise

**Athlete appeals and sport arbitration** are the primary market. When an athlete, coach, or national federation believes judging was corrupt or anomalous, they need expert witnesses who can quantify the anomaly statistically and explain it to a tribunal. Your OSNR framework — which produces exact p-values and counterfactual outcomes — is precisely the kind of analysis that arbitrators can evaluate.

Specific case types:

- **Court of Arbitration for Sport (CAS) appeals:** Athletes appealing judging decisions, federations challenging results, disciplinary cases involving accused judges. CAS hears hundreds of cases annually; judging disputes are a recurring category in subjectively scored sports.
- **Internal federation disciplinary proceedings:** The ISU, FIG (gymnastics), FIS (ski jumping/freestyle), and IJF (judo) all have internal hearing panels when judges are accused of misconduct. These panels can hire outside statistical experts.
- **National Olympic Committee disputes:** When a national team believes their athlete was wrongly judged at an Olympic Games, the NOC may pursue remedies through CAS or direct IOC appeal. The 2002 Salt Lake City figure skating scandal is the archetype.
- **Civil litigation:** Less common but not impossible — in jurisdictions where sporting decisions can be challenged in civil court, statistical evidence of anomalous judging could support claims of tortious interference or breach of competition rules.
- **Criminal proceedings:** In extreme cases involving proven bribery (as in 2002), statistical analysis of scoring patterns becomes evidence of the scheme's execution.

### How the Court of Arbitration for Sport Works

CAS is based in Lausanne, Switzerland, with hearing centers in New York and Sydney. It functions like commercial arbitration: parties select arbitrators from a list, hearings are conducted in writing and sometimes in person, decisions are binding (with limited appeal to Swiss Federal Tribunal).

Who the clients are in judging cases:
- Athletes or their agents (often the party most motivated to appeal)
- National Olympic Committees funding the athlete's appeal
- Sport federations defending or challenging decisions
- Insurance companies covering competition outcomes

Attorneys who practice before CAS are often at specialized sports law firms (examples: Latham & Watkins sports practice, Morgan Lewis, specialized firms like Schindler Attorneys or Lévy Kaufmann-Kohler). These are the attorneys you want to know.

### How to Position Yourself

Your positioning statement: "I am the developer of the OSNR (Outlier Score Nullification Rule) framework, a peer-reviewed permutation-based statistical method for detecting outcome-determinative judging anomalies in competitive figure skating. My audit of 142 events and 291,604 individual judge scores is the largest published dataset of its kind."

Key credibility signals for legal work:
- Peer-reviewed publication (Scientific Reports — high-impact, open access, Nature Portfolio — this is essential)
- Open data and reproducible code on GitHub (lawyers and opposing experts can verify your methods)
- MBA from University of Chicago Booth (quantitative business credibility, not just academic)
- Specific numerical outputs: exact p-values, counterfactual rankings, outcome-determinative flags

### How to Get Found by Attorneys

1. **Expert witness directories.** Register with SEAK (seak.com), JurisExpert, and the National Expert Witness Directory once your paper is published. These directories are where attorneys search. Cost is modest ($200–$500/year).

2. **Sports law bar associations.** The Sports Lawyers Association (SLA) holds an annual conference. Submit a paper or attend. The attorneys in the room handle exactly the cases you want. The American Bar Association's Forum on Entertainment and Sports Industries is another entry point.

3. **Write for sports law publications.** The Journal of Legal Aspects of Sport, the Marquette Sports Law Review, and the International Sports Law Journal all publish practitioner-relevant articles. A short article titled something like "Statistical Methods for Evaluating Judging Anomalies: Implications for CAS Practitioners" would be read by exactly the attorneys who need you.

4. **LinkedIn.** Once your paper is published, write a post announcing it that explicitly mentions CAS, judging appeals, and expert witness availability. Sports law attorneys are active on LinkedIn.

5. **Reach out directly to sports law firms.** A short, professional email to the sports practice group at 5–10 relevant firms — attaching your paper abstract and noting expert witness availability — is not unusual. Attorneys maintain expert witness rosters; they want to know you exist before they have a case.

**Important:** Expert witness work is episodic. You may go six months with no inquiries, then have two cases arrive simultaneously. Do not count on it as primary income in year one.

## 5. Book Strategy

### The Trade Book Opportunity

Your research has genuine trade book potential. The story elements are all present:

- Olympics (the world's biggest sporting stage)
- A single corrupt judge (a villain)
- Statistical sleuthing (the method)
- A result that was stolen (the stakes)
- A system that could be fixed (the solution)

This is the structure of a successful narrative nonfiction sports book. Think Michael Lewis's approach applied to Olympic judging.

### Target Audience

Primary: General readers interested in sports, the Olympics, and corruption/fairness narratives. The audience for books like *Moneyball*, *The Billion Dollar Game*, *Soccernomics*, and *The Numbers Game*.

Secondary: Sports analytics professionals, statisticians, governance researchers, figure skating fans.

### Comparable Titles

- *Moneyball* (Lewis) — analytics disrupting a traditional sports establishment
- *The Fix* (Declan Hill) — match-fixing in soccer, investigative narrative
- *Scorecasting* (Moskowitz and Wertheim) — referee bias and sports statistics for a general audience
- *Soccernomics* (Kuper and Szymanski) — applying economic analysis to sports
- *The Miracle and Tragedy of the Dionne Quintuplets* — not sports, but shows how a statistical anomaly can anchor narrative nonfiction

Your book is closest to *Scorecasting* in method but has a more dramatic central finding and a natural villain (the anomalous judge).

### Academic Book vs. Trade Book

An **academic book** (published by MIT Press, Princeton University Press, University of Chicago Press) is primarily for scholars and graduate students. Advances are modest ($2,000–$10,000), print runs are small (1,000–3,000 copies), and royalties are low. The prestige value is real for an academic career but limited for your monetization goals.

A **trade book** (published by Simon & Schuster, Penguin Random House, Norton, Hachette, etc.) is for general readers. Advances for first-time nonfiction authors with a strong hook range from $20,000–$100,000, with meaningful upside if the book sells. This is where you should aim.

A hybrid option: W.W. Norton's general science/nonfiction list, or MIT Press's popular science list, sits between trade and academic. These can work well for data-driven narrative books.

### How to Find a Literary Agent

You need a literary agent to approach major trade publishers. Agents take 15% of advances and royalties in exchange for placing your book and negotiating your contract.

**How to find agents:**

1. **QueryTracker (querytracker.net):** Database of literary agents with submission guidelines and track records.
2. **Publishers Marketplace (publishersmarketplace.com):** Shows recent deals by agent and genre. Search for agents who have sold sports narrative nonfiction, science journalism, or corruption/exposé books. These are the agents who will understand your proposal.
3. **Writer's Digest Guide to Literary Agents:** The standard annual reference.
4. **Conferences:** The Association of Writers and Writing Programs (AWP) conference and various literary festivals include agent pitch sessions. Less critical for nonfiction (proposal-driven) than fiction.

**Agents to research** (examples of agents who have represented similar work — research current status and current submissions):
- Agents at CAA, WME, and Janklow & Nesbit who represent narrative sports nonfiction
- Agents who represented *The Fix*, *Scorecasting*, or similar titles
- Search Publishers Marketplace for "sports" + "nonfiction" deals from 2022–2026

### The Book Proposal

Trade publishers buy nonfiction on proposal, not completed manuscript. A book proposal is 20–50 pages including:

- Overview (the hook, the story, why now)
- Chapter-by-chapter outline
- Sample chapter (usually the most compelling narrative section)
- Market analysis (comparable titles and their sales)
- Author bio (your Booth MBA, your paper, your platform)

Your strongest sample chapter is almost certainly the OWG 2026 ice dance story told as narrative: the competition, the scores, the statistical anomaly, the counterfactual medal table, and what it means.

### Working Title Ideas

## 8. Realistic Expectations

### The Honest Timeline

First papers do not immediately produce income. They produce credibility, which produces opportunities, which (eventually) produce income. The sequence is predictable even if the timing varies:

1. Paper published → media coverage (weeks to months)
2. Media coverage → speaking invitations (months)
3. Speaking appearances → consulting inquiries (months to a year)
4. Consulting relationships → retained work and referrals (1–2 years)
5. Book deal → advance payment and profile transformation (2–4 years)

Expert witness cases: Plan for 12–24 months before the first serious inquiry from an attorney, assuming you have done the marketing work (directories, sports law article, website). Cases emerge unpredictably — you cannot force them, only make yourself findable.

First speaking fee: Realistically 6–12 months after publication for a modest paid engagement. The first few appearances will be unpaid or travel-only. This is normal and necessary.

### The Compound Effect

Every publication leads to more publications. Every speaking appearance leads to more invitations. Every media quote generates more media requests. Every consulting engagement produces referrals. The curve is slow in year one and accelerating in years two through five.

The researchers who successfully monetize a first paper are the ones who treat marketing and outreach as a second full-time job in years one and two. The academic reflex is to publish and wait to be discovered. The entrepreneurial approach — which your Booth MBA prepares you for — is to publish and actively distribute.

### What Sets You Apart
