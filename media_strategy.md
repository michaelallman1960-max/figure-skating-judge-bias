## Overview and Guiding Principles

This document is a practical, sequenced strategy for communicating findings from a statistical audit of figure skating judging to academic, journalistic, and public audiences. The findings are genuinely newsworthy — a single judge's scores at the OWG 2026 ice dance event were statistically anomalous at p = 0.0003, and removing that judge from the calculation would have changed the gold medal outcome. This is not a borderline result. The story tells itself; the job of this strategy is to control timing, framing, and channels so that the message lands with maximum credibility and minimum distortion.

**Three governing principles:**

1. **Credibility first.** Everything flows from the paper. Do not get ahead of peer review in claims, but do not wait for peer review to begin positioning. The research is solid; the communication strategy should reflect that confidence without overclaiming.

2. **The OWG 2026 window is short.** Milano-Cortina just concluded. Journalistic interest in figure skating and Olympic judging controversies will peak in the next 4–8 weeks and then decline sharply. The communication strategy must move quickly without sacrificing accuracy.

3. **Own the independent researcher narrative.** The absence of institutional affiliation is not a weakness to apologize for — it is the story. An MBA-trained independent researcher, using modern AI tools, built an audit framework rigorous enough to detect what ISU's own system did not. That is a compelling angle and should be foregrounded consistently.

### Language Caution for Pre-Peer-Review Op-Eds

Before peer review is complete, use language such as:
- "My analysis found..."
- "The data indicate..."
- "Under this statistical framework..."
- "A paper describing this methodology is under review at..."

Avoid:
- "Proves" (replace with "provides strong statistical evidence")
- "The judge was biased" (replace with "the judge's scores were statistically anomalous")
- "Cheating" (this is a legal and moral claim; use "anomalous," "irregular," or "outlying")

### Op-Ed Template Structure (NYT-style)

**Paragraph 1 (The Lede):** Start with the moment — the ice dance final at Milano-Cortina, the gold medal announced, the scores published. Set the scene without accusation. Then pivot: for the first time, we have the tools to ask a question that used to be unanswerable. One judge's scores were so far outside the statistical norm that removing them from the calculation produces a different winner — and the probability of that pattern arising by chance is roughly 1 in 3,333.

**Paragraph 2 (The Opportunity — the author's framing):** This is not primarily a story of wrongdoing. It is a story of what is now possible. Technology has created an opportunity to verify the integrity of judged sports in real time, conservatively, and transparently. The question is whether governing bodies will seize it. *[Key framing: forward-looking, hopeful, about improvement — not accusation.]*

**Paragraphs 3–4 (The Method, briefly):** No formulas. One sentence: "I developed a statistical test that asks a simple question: if this judge were removed from the panel, would the result change?" The answer, in the OWG 2026 ice dance case: yes — and the probability this was random variation is roughly 1 in 3,333. Crucially: the test is conservative by design. It flags only cases where the anomaly is both statistically extreme and outcome-determinative.

**Paragraphs 5–6 (The Systemic Finding):** This is not just one case. Across 17 competitions and 142 events, 46 events had at least one judge whose removal would have reshuffled the podium. The system has structural vulnerabilities. But those vulnerabilities are now detectable — and detectable in real time, before results are announced.

**Paragraph 7 (The Fix):** The audit framework is fully reproducible, open-source, and could be applied immediately. Real-time. Conservative — it does not overturn results unless the anomaly is both statistically extreme and outcome-changing. The ISU could implement this in a single competition season. There is no technical barrier.

**Paragraph 8 (The Close):** I built this alone, working independently, in a week. The ISU has had decades and the data has always been public. The tools now exist. The moment for governing bodies to choose transparency and confidence over institutional inertia is now — while the Games have just ended and the world is still watching.

## Section 2: Journalism / Press Strategy

### The Journalism Goal

Press coverage differs from op-eds: you are a source, not the author. The journalist controls the framing. Your goal is to find journalists who will tell an accurate, rigorous version of the story — not to maximize the number of outlets that cover it. One well-done piece in a major outlet with your data and quotes is worth more than ten poorly framed stories.

### When to Reach Out to Journalists

**Start now.** The OWG 2026 news cycle is the peg. Journalists covering Milan-Cortina are still filing stories. The figure skating judging controversies from the Games (and there are always some — the community perceived several questionable results in 2026) are live topics. Your findings are directly relevant.

**Do not wait for peer review acceptance.** Most sports journalism does not require peer-reviewed publication. Frame it accurately: "My research is currently under review at [journal]. The data and methodology are publicly available." Journalists cover findings under review all the time — they just need to note the caveat.

**Embargo option:** Consider offering a 48-hour embargo to 2–3 journalists simultaneously. Send the paper and data, ask them to hold publication until a set date, and then release simultaneously. This creates urgency and ensures coverage lands together rather than trickling. Only do this if you have multiple journalists interested — do not embargo to a single outlet.

### Priority Outlets by Category

**Sports Data Journalism (highest priority):**
- The Athletic: Largest audience for in-depth sports coverage; reporters who understand data. Find reporters who covered figure skating at OWG 2026.
- ESPN Analytics: ESPN has published figure skating judging pieces before. Reach out to their investigative or feature reporters.
- Sports Illustrated: Less data-forward but large audience; good for the narrative version.

**Wire Services (for broad distribution):**
- Associated Press Sports Desk: If AP covers it, the story appears in hundreds of outlets simultaneously. This is extremely high-value. AP reporters covering figure skating are the target. Find who filed AP copy from Milano-Cortina.
- Reuters Sports: Similar to AP; strong international reach, which matters because this story affects multiple national federations.

**Olympic and Figure Skating Beat Reporters:**

- These reporters also know which national federations are likely to respond, giving you advance warning of pushback.

**General Investigative / Accountability:**
- ProPublica: If you want to frame this as a governance failure and accountability story (which it partly is), ProPublica is interested in exactly this type of data-driven institutional audit. They have partnered with academics and independent researchers before.
- NYT Investigations or Sports desks (separate from Opinion): A pitch to the NYT sports desk is different from an op-ed submission. They will want to do their own reporting. Be prepared to share full data and methodology.

### What to Send Journalists

**Do not send the full paper cold.** Journalists do not read academic papers unless they already want the story. Instead, prepare a **press packet** containing:

1. **One-page executive summary** (non-technical): The key finding, why it matters, who you are. Written at a general newspaper level. No jargon.
2. **Key statistics, pre-formatted:** A cheat sheet of the numbers journalists will want to quote:
   - 142 events audited across 17 competitions
   - 2,706 competitor entries; 291,604 individual judge scores analyzed
   - 264,854 pairwise comparisons
   - 1,774 statistically significant anomalous judge-competitor pairs (p ≤ 0.001)
   - 46 events where removing one judge changes the podium
   - OWG 2026 ice dance: p = 0.0003 (1 in approximately 3,333)
3. **The paper itself** (attached as PDF, clearly labeled as "under review at [journals]")
4. **GitHub link** to open data and code
5. **Your contact information** and availability for calls/interviews

### How to Frame the Story for Non-Technical Audiences

The single most important translation: **"p = 0.0003" = "a 1-in-3,333 chance this happened by random variation."**

Pre-cooked comparisons that work for journalists:
- "More statistically unusual than flipping a coin 14 times and getting heads every time."
- "If you ran this same competition 3,333 times with randomly assigned judges, you would expect to see scores this extreme approximately once."

Avoid:
- Saying "the judge was biased" (you cannot prove intent; say "the scores were anomalous")
- Saying "the gold medal was stolen" (inflammatory, legally risky, overstates what statistics can show)
- Saying "I can prove fraud" (you cannot; you can prove statistical irregularity)

### Soundbites / Quotable Lines

Prepare these in advance. Journalists will ask for a quote; having these ready ensures accuracy:

- "The test asks one question: if you removed this judge from the panel and reran the competition, does the winner change? For the OWG 2026 ice dance event, the answer is yes — and the probability that this happened by chance is about 1 in 3,333."
- "I am not accusing anyone of anything. I am showing that the current scoring system has structural vulnerabilities that can be detected mathematically and that the ISU has not applied any systematic audit to identify them."
- "This is not a complex research tool. It is a combinatorial permutation test — a standard statistical technique — applied to a dataset that should have been audited years ago."
- "An independent researcher with no budget and no institutional support built this audit framework in a week. The ISU has had the data and the resources for decades."
- "Open data, open code, fully reproducible. Any statistician in the world can check my work."

### What Journalists Will Ask — and How to Answer

**"Are you naming the judge?"**
Answer: "My paper reports results at the event and judge-position level. I have not publicly named specific judges, and I would be cautious about doing so outside of a formal investigative process. The statistical finding is about a pattern in scores, not a proven intention."

**"Are you saying the gold medalist didn't deserve to win?"**
Answer: "I am saying that under the scoring system that was actually used, one judge's scores were statistically anomalous in a way that changed the result. Whether a different outcome would have been more or less 'deserved' is a judgment the sport's governing body needs to make — that is why I am calling for a formal review process."

**"Why should we trust your findings if they haven't been peer-reviewed?"**
Answer: "The paper is under review at [journals]. In the meantime, the data and code are publicly available on GitHub. I am actively inviting any statistician to replicate or challenge the results. I have nothing to hide, and neither does the math."

**"What do you want to happen?"**
Answer: "I would like to see the ISU adopt a systematic real-time audit process — which this framework can provide — so that these anomalies are caught before results are announced rather than months later. The technology exists. The question is institutional will."

**"Are you politically motivated? Is this about [country]?"**
Answer: "The findings cover 17 competitions and implicate judges from multiple countries and multiple events. This is not a story about any one nationality or federation. The statistical test does not care what flag a judge is from."

## Section 4: Figure Skating Community Outreach

### ISU and National Federations

**Philosophy:** Inform, do not attack. Notify before publication if possible. This is both strategically smart and ethically correct.

**ISU notification:** Send a brief letter (email to ISU Communications and/or the Secretary General's office) 48–72 hours before the paper goes live, informing them that research is being published, providing an abstract and the key finding, and offering to discuss the methodology. Do not ask permission. Do not ask them to suppress the findings. Simply notify them professionally.

This approach:
- Demonstrates good faith, which protects you from accusations of ambush journalism
- Creates a paper trail showing you attempted engagement
- Gives them an opportunity to comment, which makes press coverage more balanced (journalists will ask "did you contact ISU?" — you will be able to say yes)
- May initiate a dialogue that leads to institutional adoption of the framework

**What to say to ISU:** Frame as a contribution to the sport, not an indictment. "I have developed a statistical audit framework and applied it to publicly available competition data. The findings identify events where a single judge's scores were statistically anomalous in ways that affected outcomes. I am making this public in the interest of the sport's integrity and credibility. I would welcome a conversation about how this framework could be used constructively."

**National federation awareness:** Consider notifying the federations whose athletes were directly affected by the OWG 2026 ice dance finding. Both the federation whose athlete received the gold medal under current scoring AND the federation whose athlete would have received gold under the adjusted scoring have legitimate interests. Notify both simultaneously, with the same neutral framing.

Do not notify one and not the other. Do not provide preferential information to any federation.

### Figure Skating Fan Community

The fan community is your most natural ally and your greatest amplification risk simultaneously.

**Natural allies:** Figure skating fans have followed judging controversies since the 2002 Salt Lake City scandal. They understand the IJS system, track judge assignments, and have long suspected systematic anomalies. They will immediately understand what the OWG 2026 ice dance finding means. Many will amplify your work energetically.

**The risk:** Fan communities can quickly move from "this judge's scores were anomalous" to "this judge is corrupt and should be banned" to "this country's skaters should have their medals revoked." This escalation is harmful to your credibility and to the skaters involved, who are not responsible for judging decisions.

**How to engage the fan community:**
- Post in major figure skating fan forums (GoldenSkate, Reddit r/FigureSkating) with a clear, measured presentation of the findings.
- Explicitly state what the findings do and do not show. "Statistically anomalous" does not mean "corrupt." "Outcome-determinative" does not mean "the gold medal should be transferred."
- Provide the GitHub link and encourage people to verify the numbers themselves.
- Engage politely with questions; disengage from harassment or demands to name judges.

### Athletes and Coaches

Some athletes and coaches will be very interested in findings that directly affect their competitive results. Handle this carefully.

**Direct outreach:** Do not reach out to athletes or coaches individually about findings that affect their specific results before publication. This would be inappropriate — they should learn about findings affecting them from official channels or press coverage, not from you cold-contacting them.

**After publication:** If athletes or coaches reach out to you, respond professionally and factually. Offer to walk them through the methodology. Do not speculate about what the findings "should" mean for their competitive history.

**Coaching/federation community:** Coaches at senior ISU events are sophisticated about judging patterns and will find the audit framework professionally interesting. After publication, consider whether presenting at coaches' summits or federation technical meetings would be appropriate.

### Message 1: The System Has a Detectable Flaw — and I Detected It

## Section 7: Risk Management

### ISU Pushback or Legal Threats

**Most likely ISU response:** Silence, or a brief statement that "the ISU's judging processes comply with all relevant rules and regulations." This is the standard response to external statistical critiques of sports governing bodies.

**Less likely but possible:** A formal statement disputing the methodology or conclusions. This is actually useful for you — it creates a public debate in which you hold the technical high ground (open data, reproducible methods, peer review underway).

**Unlikely but prepare for:** Legal pressure, including cease-and-desist letters or threats of defamation litigation.

**If you receive legal threats:**
- Do not respond directly to legal correspondence without legal counsel
- The strongest defense against defamation claims is truth and good faith. Your findings are based on publicly available data and are fully reproducible. You have been careful to describe what the data shows, not make moral or legal claims about specific individuals.
- Avoid naming specific judges. The paper reports by event and judge panel position, not by individual name. This is both methodologically appropriate (the finding is about the scoring pattern, not the person) and legally protective.
- Contact a First Amendment attorney or academic freedom organization (even as an independent researcher, organizations like the Reporters Committee for Freedom of the Press can provide guidance on research that implicates powerful sports governing bodies).
- Publicize any legal threats. The skating community and press will find ISU attempting to suppress statistical research far more damaging to ISU than the research itself. Legal threats against independent researchers doing reproducible data analysis on publicly available information almost always backfire on the threatening party.

### Accusations of Sensationalism

**The accusation:** "You are cherry-picking the most dramatic finding (OWG 2026 ice dance) to generate headlines rather than reporting the full picture."

**The answer:** The full picture is in the paper and the GitHub repository. Every calculation for every event is available. The OWG 2026 ice dance finding is highlighted because it is the most statistically extreme — that is not cherry-picking, that is reporting the most important finding. The other 6 outcome-determinative events are reported in full.

**Preparation:** Make sure the paper and GitHub repository genuinely do report all findings in full, including cases where the ISU-impact framework found nothing anomalous. If you only report positive findings, the sensationalism accusation has more force.

### Requests to Name Specific Judges or Athletes

**Journalists, fans, and commenters will pressure you to name names.** Some journalists will feel the story requires naming to be concrete; some fans will feel justice requires it.

**The principled position:** Statistical analysis can identify anomalous scoring patterns at the judge panel position level. It cannot — and should not — publicly convict specific individuals of wrongdoing. Naming a judge in a press context is potentially defamatory if they have not had the opportunity to respond through a formal process. The appropriate forum for naming and investigating specific individuals is a formal ISU review process, not a media campaign.

**What you can say:** "The paper identifies the event, the approximate magnitude of the anomaly, and the statistical parameters. The identity of specific judges is part of the official record held by ISU. Any investigation should be conducted by the appropriate governing body with due process protections."

**What you should not do:** Include judge names in social media posts, press interviews, or op-eds, even if journalists press you or fan communities are certain they know who it is. You can acknowledge the judge's identity is in ISU's own published records without pointing to it yourself.

### Accusations of National or Political Bias

Given that the OWG 2026 ice dance finding affects specific countries' athletes, you may face accusations that your research is motivated by national sympathies.

**Preparation:**
- Emphasize that the framework is fully algorithmic and neutral. The code does not know or care about nationalities.
- Point to the full dataset: 46 outcome-determinative events across 17 competitions, involving many countries and national federations.
- Be prepared to name which countries and events appeared frequently in the findings — if the full dataset shows balance across multiple federations, that is your strongest defense.
- If your own national background is raised, respond simply: "My country's athletes appear in this dataset. The method does not distinguish. If there were anomalous findings involving judges favorable to my country's athletes, the framework would have identified them too."

### Handling Low-Quality Press Coverage

Not all journalists will cover this accurately. Some will overstate findings, some will understate them, some will sensationalize, and some will get the statistics wrong.

**Strategy:** You cannot control every story. Focus energy on the outlets most likely to do it well (The Athletic, AP wire reporters who cover skating, academic/science journalists at major papers). A few accurate, detailed pieces are worth more than widespread shallow coverage.

If a major outlet runs a story with material errors, send a polite correction to the reporter and the editor. Keep the correction factual and non-confrontational. Public corrections are sometimes appropriate on social media, framed as "I want to clarify what the data actually show" rather than "this reporter got it wrong."
