I'd like to pitch a bylined data explainer (1,200–1,500 words) on how standard statistical techniques, applied to publicly available data, can detect when a figure skating judge's scores are anomalous enough to change an Olympic result.

**The hook:** At the 2026 Olympic ice dance final, one judge on the nine-judge panel scored so far outside the statistical norm that removing that single judge and recomputing under ISU rules flips the gold and silver medals. **The explainer angle:** The method is a combinatorial permutation test — conceptually simple, computationally exact, and applicable far beyond figure skating. Take the scores from two competitors across all scored elements. Hold them fixed. Ask: of every possible way those scores could have been distributed between the two judges, how many produce a disagreement as extreme as the one actually observed? The answer is an exact fraction — no simulation, no random seed, no distributional assumption. It's the kind of technique that could audit any judged sport, from gymnastics to diving to boxing.
